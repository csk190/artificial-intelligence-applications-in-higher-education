# Systematic Review of AI Applications in Higher Education – Zawacki-Richter et al. (2019)

## 1. Why this review?
- AI in Education (AIEd) is rapidly growing, yet educators lack clear guidance on how to use it pedagogically.
- Most AIEd research is led by Computer Science and STEM fields — educational perspectives are largely missing.
- Ethical implications and risks of AIEd are critically under-examined in existing literature.

## 2. Research questions
- How have AIEd publications developed over time, and where do they come from geographically and disciplinarily?
- How is AI conceptualised, and what ethical challenges and risks are considered?
- What is the nature and scope of AI applications in higher education?

## 3. Method
- **Systematic review** of peer-reviewed journal articles published between 2007–2018.
- Databases searched: EBSCO Education Source, Web of Science, Scopus.
- 2,656 records initially identified → **146 articles** included for final synthesis.
- Inter-rater reliability (Cohen's kappa): median κ = .79 (excellent).

## 4. Key descriptive findings
- Publications grew from **6 articles (2007)** to **23 articles (2018)**.
- Top journals: _International Journal of AI in Education_ (n=11), _Computers & Education_ (n=8).
- **50% of all articles** came from just 4 countries: USA, China, Taiwan, Turkey.
- **62%** of first authors were from Computer Science or STEM departments.
- Only **8.9%** of first authors had an Education background.
- **73.3%** of empirical studies used quantitative methods; only 0.7% were qualitative.

## 5. Four areas of AIEd applications⭐
| Application Area | Key Sub-categories | Level |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Profiling & Prediction** | Admission decisions, drop-out & retention, student models & academic achievement | Institutional & Academic |
| **2. Assessment & Evaluation** | Automated grading (AES), feedback, academic integrity, evaluation of teaching | Mainly Academic |
| **3. Adaptive Systems & Personalisation** | Personalised content, teacher support, learning design, concept maps | Mainly Academic |
| **4. Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS)** | Teaching content, diagnosing gaps, curating materials, facilitating collaboration | Academic |

## 6. Notable findings by application area
1) **Profiling & Prediction** (n=58)
   - _machine learning, ANN, SVM, dropout prediction, admission decisions_
   - ML methods consistently outperformed logistic regression in prediction accuracy.
2) **Assessment & Evaluation** (n=36)
   - _automated essay scoring (AES), feedback, engagement, academic integrity_
   - AES achieved human-rater agreement of up to 98.2%, but requires large datasets to calibrate.
3) **Adaptive Systems** (n=27)
   - _personalised content, intelligent agents, recommender systems, MOOCs_
   - Most studies reported positive pilot results, but lacked longitudinal follow-up.
4) **Intelligent Tutoring Systems** (n=29)
   - _ITS, chatbots, virtual labs, dialogue agents_
   - ITS showed moderate learning effects; less effective than human tutoring but outperformed traditional instruction.

## 7. Critical concerns🔑
- Only **3.4%** of articles (5 out of 146) explicitly defined "Artificial Intelligence."
- Only **1.4%** (2 articles) critically reflected on ethical implications or risks.
- Privacy, data protection, and surveillance risks are almost entirely absent from the literature.
- Strong lack of pedagogical theory — over 40% of educational technology articles are wholly a-theoretical.
- Longitudinal studies are nearly nonexistent; most are descriptive or short-term pilot studies.

## 8. Conclusions and implications
- AI applications hold enormous potential across the student lifecycle (support, assessment, tutoring, administration).
- Educational technology is **not only about technology** — pedagogical, ethical, social, and cultural dimensions matter.
- More involvement from **educators and learning designers** is urgently needed in AIEd research.
- Researchers should be explicit about the **theoretical foundations** underpinning their AIEd studies.
- An **ethics of care** must guide the development and implementation of AI in education.
- AIEd systems require human oversight: "AI systems are only as smart as the data used to train them."
